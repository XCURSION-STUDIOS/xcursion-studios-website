The Almanack of Naval Ravikant —
the parts that actually stuck

November 20245 min read

I do not read books the way I am supposed to. I graze. I skip chapters. I come back months later and find passages I do not remember reading but have clearly absorbed. The Almanack of Naval Ravikant is the kind of book that works well for this. It is not a narrative. It is a collection of compressed ideas, and some of them are very good.

The Ideas That Stayed

Specific knowledge — the idea that the thing that makes you valuable cannot be trained. It is the weird combination of interests and experiences that is uniquely yours. You do not build it deliberately. You follow your curiosity and one day realise you know something no one else quite knows in the same way.

Escape competition through authenticity. No one can compete with you on being you.

This sounds obvious. It is not. Most people spend enormous energy trying to be better at the same things as everyone else. The returns on differentiation are far higher than the returns on incremental improvement in a crowded field.

What Felt Like Play

The other idea I keep coming back to: if it feels like work to you and play to someone else, they will outcompete you. Not because they are more talented, but because they will put in more hours without noticing. The person who is genuinely delighted by what they do has an almost unfair advantage.

I think about this with writing. Some people find it excruciating. I find it clarifying. Not always easy, but the struggle feels productive rather than punishing. That asymmetry matters.

The book is free online. Read it in pieces. Let it sit. Some of it will not land until later, and that is fine.
