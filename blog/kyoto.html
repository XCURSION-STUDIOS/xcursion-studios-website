Travel

Finding stillness
in Kyoto

February 20258 min read

I arrived in Kyoto with a spreadsheet. Seventeen items. Each with a time window, a transit route, and a backup. I had optimised the trip before it began.

By day two, I had abandoned the spreadsheet somewhere between Fushimi Inari and a conversation with an elderly man feeding koi in a garden I hadn't planned to visit.

The City That Doesn't Hurry

There's something about Kyoto that resists your urgency. The temples aren't competing for your attention. The streets don't escalate. The food arrives slowly and tastes better for it. The city just exists, steadily, as it has for centuries, completely indifferent to whether you ‘got the most out of it’.

"The journey is not the distance between two points. It's the accumulation of things you noticed on the way."

On day three I stopped taking photos for two hours. Just walked. Got lost twice. Found a paper shop with a wooden floor and incense burning from somewhere I couldn't locate. Bought a single sheet of indigo washi paper I have no practical use for. It's still the best purchase of the trip.

What Slow Travel Actually Means

I used to think ‘slow travel’ meant spending longer in one place. It turns out it means spending less time planning what to do in one place. The difference is everything.

Kyoto taught me that there's a version of travel where the city wins. Where you stop trying to extract maximum experience per hour and instead just let the place exist around you. The bamboo grove at Arashiyama was beautiful. But it was most beautiful at 7am before anyone else arrived, standing there with no intention except to look at it.

I'm writing this on a flight home with no notes open and no photos selected for editing. For once I don't feel like I missed anything. Which might be the whole point.
