Hollow Knight and the
philosophy of getting lost

January 20256 min read

I am very bad at Hollow Knight. I want to put that on record before anything else. I have died to the same boss eleven times. I have no idea where I am on the map. I am playing a game that does not tell me where to go, and I am enjoying it more than almost anything I have played in years.

A Game Built on Not Knowing

Hollow Knight does not hold your hand. It does not put a glowing marker on the thing you are supposed to do next. It drops you into a decaying insect kingdom and trusts that you will find your way through a combination of curiosity, frustration, and increasingly careful movement.

The map is incomplete. The lore is fragmented. The world was here before you and it will continue without you. You are just passing through.

What strikes me is how this matches how things actually work. Most of the interesting stuff in life does not come with a waypoint. You explore, you hit walls, you find unexpected passages. You die a lot and try again slightly differently.

Why Getting Lost Is the Point

There is a design philosophy in the game called environmental storytelling — the idea that the world communicates through what you find rather than what you are told. A cluster of graves. A locked door with no key in sight. A character who says something cryptic and disappears.

I think about this in the context of learning. The best things I know came from following something past the point where it made obvious sense. Staying in the unknown long enough for patterns to emerge. That is uncomfortable. It also seems to be the only way through.

I am still in the middle of the game. I have stopped trying to find the critical path. I am just exploring. Which, as far as I can tell, is exactly what it is asking me to do.
